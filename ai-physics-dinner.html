AI x Physics Dinner

We gathered physicists, AI researchers, and builders to explore the deep connections between physics and machine learning. The conversations ranged from thermodynamic computing to tensor networks, from the foundations of optimization to the future of energy-efficient AI.

Ideas from the Evening

Thermodynamic Computing: Training Neural Networks with Physics

One of the most provocative ideas discussed was using thermodynamic systems to train neural networks without traditional backpropagation. Instead of simulating gradient descent on digital hardware, you can build analog systems governed by Kirchhoff's laws where the physics itself performs the optimization.

The key insight: in a network of resistors with thermal noise, the fluctuations at each node contain information about how to update the weights. By clamping input and output nodes and observing how the hidden nodes fluctuate, you can derive weight updates that minimize energy through the system. The "backward pass" happens at the speed of light through the physical substrate.

The Energy Efficiency Frontier

Current digital systems operate at around 0.7 volts, but thermodynamic systems can operate at the thermal limit of kT (about 26 millielectronvolts at room temperature). This represents a potential 107 improvement in energy efficiency for neural network training. As one participant put it: "This could kick the trillion-dollar data centers in the teeth."

P-bits and the Higgs Potential

The discussion touched on using bi-stable wells (similar to the Higgs potential in particle physics) for computation. These "p-bits" (probabilistic bits) sit in a double-well potential where thermal fluctuations cause them to hop between states. The beautiful insight: even though each p-bit has very low information capacity (roughly ln(2) bits), by time-averaging over thermal fluctuations, you can recover arbitrary floating-point precision.

Non-Equilibrium Dynamics: Why This Time Is Different

Earlier attempts at analog neural computing in the late 1980s failed because they required systems to reach thermal equilibrium, which limited the class of solvable problems. The new approaches work in the non-equilibrium regime, where the system never needs to thermalize. This is a fundamental departure that opens up much richer computational possibilities.

Tensor Networks Meet Neural Networks

The conversation explored how tensor network decompositions from quantum physics can be applied to neural network architectures. This allows attention mechanisms, state machines, and other complex architectures to be expressed in forms amenable to physical implementation. The mathematical structure of tensor contraction maps naturally onto certain analog computing substrates.

Meta-Learning and Gradient-Free Optimization

Connections were drawn to the meta-learning literature from 2017-2020, where researchers explored gradient-free methods for training neural networks. These "learned optimizers" could take activations as input and produce gradient signals without explicit backpropagation. The physical computing approaches discussed share deep mathematical structure with these methods.

The Bigger Picture

The evening highlighted a broader theme: as we push toward more powerful AI systems, we're being forced to reconsider the physical substrate of computation itself. The separation between "hardware" and "algorithm" is blurring. Physics isn't just a metaphor for learning—it might be the most efficient way to actually implement it.

The conversations continued late into the night, with discussions ranging from KKLT theories in string theory to the information bottleneck principle, from diffusion models to defense applications. It was a reminder that the most interesting ideas often emerge at the intersection of fields.
