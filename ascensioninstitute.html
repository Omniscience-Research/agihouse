Coordinating capital toward the frontiers of research automation

The people building the most transformative tools in history are uniquely positioned to direct where they are applied. We exist to make that coordination happen.

The Observation

The researchers automating science are the only ones who can steer it

Engineers at the frontier AI labs sit at an unprecedented intersection: they possess both deep technical knowledge of what these systems can do, and increasingly, the financial capital to act on that knowledge.

Meanwhile, the academics best positioned to identify which problems matter most — the scientists pushing at the boundaries of biology, physics, mathematics — have the expertise but lack the resources. Many face resistance within their own institutions, fighting to adopt the very tools that could transform their fields.

These two groups need each other.

The Gap

Academia cannot lead this transition alone

Traditional institutions were designed for a different pace of discovery. Tenure incentives reward incrementalism. Funding cycles are measured in years, not the weeks it now takes for a capability to emerge. The researchers who see what is coming are working against the academic zeitgeist.

The question is not whether research will be automated. It is how deliberately we choose which systems to automate, and who is making those decisions.

The Approach

A new model for coordinating research capital

I

Identify

Find the researchers operating at the edge — those who understand what AI can do for their discipline and are pushing forward despite institutional resistance. These are the scientists whose work will define the next decade of discovery.

II

Connect

Bring together frontier AI engineers — the people with both technical depth and capital — with the researchers who know which problems are worth solving. Create the conditions for genuine intellectual exchange, not transactional networking.

III

Direct

Coordinate the flow of capital toward specific, high-leverage research automation efforts. Not broad mandates, but targeted investments in the tools and systems that will compress decades of scientific progress into years.

Focus Areas

Where the bottleneck is courage, not feasibility

Genomic Engineering & Reproductive Biology

Polygenic prediction, artificial gametogenesis, iterated meiosis, synthetic genomics. The science of creating eggs and sperm from stem cells is advancing faster than institutions can process. Full genome synthesis is no longer theoretical. The regulatory and reputational barriers dwarf the technical ones.

Neurotechnology & Cognitive Engineering

Direct neural interfaces, optogenetics, expansion microscopy, memory augmentation. The gap between what is technically possible and what is institutionally permitted is enormous. AI accelerates signal decoding and neural circuit mapping by orders of magnitude.

Longevity Biology

Treating aging as an engineering problem, not an inevitability. AI-driven drug discovery, gene therapy target identification, cellular reprogramming. Traditional funding structures are biased toward disease-specific research. The root cause remains largely unaddressed.

Automated Mathematics & Fundamental Physics

AI systems generating conjectures, proving theorems, exploring physical theories humans cannot navigate alone. Many mathematicians and physicists resist the premise that machines can contribute to discovery. The tools disagree.

Fusion & Advanced Energy

Commercially viable fusion, next-generation fission, advanced energy storage. The physics is increasingly solved. What remains is engineering at scale — and the will to fund approaches that operate outside traditional energy research timelines.
